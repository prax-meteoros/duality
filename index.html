What physics learned about the nature of everything

Chapter One
What Is a Quantum State?

Before we talk about waves and particles and the strangeness that lies between them, we need a word for something more fundamental.

A quantum state is the complete description of a quantum system. Everything that can be known about a photon, an electron, an atom — every measurable property it might reveal — is encoded in its quantum state.

But here is the first departure from the world as you know it: a quantum state does not tell you what something is. It tells you what something might be, and with what likelihood.

In classical physics, a state is a fact: this ball is here, moving at this speed.
In quantum physics, a state is a catalogue of possibilities — with each possibility weighted by a probability amplitude.

Think of it like this. A coin on a table is heads or tails. A classical state. But a coin spinning in the air — before it lands — is neither. It exists in a state that contains both outcomes, and the outcome only becomes definite when the coin lands. When you look.

The quantum state is the spinning coin. The mathematical object that holds all possibilities in suspension before the world forces a choice.

Ket notation

Physicists write quantum states using a notation invented by Paul Dirac, with angular brackets that look like this:

|ψ⟩
A quantum state — "ket psi"

That symbol — |ψ⟩ — is not a position. Not a velocity. Not a number. It is an abstract vector in an abstract space, and it contains, in compressed form, every possible outcome of every possible measurement you could perform on the system.

This is where the strangeness begins. Not with duality. Not with entanglement. With the simple, extraordinary fact that nature does not always commit to a single answer until asked.

Chapter Two
Wave–Particle Duality

The particle

Newton believed light was made of tiny corpuscles — particles, shot out like bullets from a source. Discrete. Countable. Here, not there.

A particle is certainty. It has a position. A trajectory. You can point to it and say: there it is.

In 1905, Einstein showed that light strikes metal and knocks out electrons one at a time — as if it were throwing punches. Not a continuous stream, but individual packets of energy he called quanta.

The photoelectric effect. Light arrives in packets — photons.
Each one carries a precise amount of energy: E = hf.
No half-photons. No fractions. Whole, or nothing.

When you detect light, it always arrives as a particle. A single click on a detector. A dot on a screen. One thing, in one place, at one time.

The wave

But Thomas Young, a century before Einstein, had already proved something troubling.

He sent light through two narrow slits and watched what appeared on the other side: not two bright lines — but a pattern of many lines. Bright bands where the light reinforced itself. Dark bands where it cancelled itself out.

Interference. Only waves do that.

A wave is possibility. It doesn't choose a path — it takes all paths. It spreads, bends around corners, passes through gaps, and where it meets itself, it creates patterns of extraordinary beauty.

Constructive interference: crest meets crest. Amplification.
Destructive interference: crest meets trough. Silence.
Two things, overlapping, creating something new.

When light travels, it moves like a wave. Not here or there, but everywhere at once — a shimmering field of probability, undulating through space.

The paradox

So which is it?

Here is where the universe refuses to simplify itself for us.

Light is not a particle. Light is not a wave. Light is both. Not alternating. Not compromising. Both, completely, at the same time.

When you look for a particle, you find one. When you look for a wave, you find one. The question you ask determines the answer you get.

De Broglie's insight

In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed something audacious: this duality doesn't just apply to light. It applies to everything. Every particle of matter — every electron, every atom, every molecule — has a wavelength.

λ = h / p
De Broglie wavelength — matter has a wave nature

The heavier something is, the shorter its wavelength — so short that for a baseball or a human being, it is immeasurably small. But for an electron, the wavelength is enormous relative to its size. Electrons diffract. They interfere. They behave like waves.

Duality is not a quirk of light. It is a property of existence itself.

See it yourself

The double-slit experiment — the most beautiful experiment in physics. Photons are fired one at a time through two slits. Watch what builds on the screen.

Double-slit experiment

When no one watches which slit the photon passes through, the particles build an interference pattern — as if each single photon went through both slits simultaneously and interfered with itself.

But the moment you place a detector at the slits — the moment you observe — the interference vanishes. Two clean bands. Particles behaving like particles. As if they knew they were being watched.

This is not a metaphor. This is what happens. Every time. Without exception.

"Anyone who is not shocked by quantum theory has not understood it."
Niels Bohr
Chapter Three
Superposition

The double-slit experiment hinted at something that the mathematics would soon make undeniable: before measurement, a quantum system does not exist in one state or another. It exists in a superposition — a combination of multiple states simultaneously.

This is not a gap in our knowledge. The electron is not secretly going through slit A or slit B while we aren't looking. The mathematics — and every experiment ever performed — insists that it goes through both. Not "both in our ignorance." Both, literally, in reality.

|ψ⟩ = α|A⟩ + β|B⟩
A superposition of two states — A and B coexist

The coefficients α and β are complex numbers called probability amplitudes. Their squared magnitudes give the probabilities of finding the system in state A or B when measured. But between measurements, both terms are real. Both are present. The system is genuinely, physically, in both states at once.

Schrödinger's cat

Erwin Schrödinger invented his famous thought experiment not to celebrate superposition, but to show how absurd it seemed. A cat in a box, its fate tied to a radioactive atom: if the atom decays, the cat dies. Quantum mechanics says the atom is in a superposition of decayed and not-decayed, and so, absurdly, the cat should be simultaneously alive and dead.

The cat was meant as a reductio ad absurdum. But nature didn't flinch. At the quantum scale, superposition is not absurd. It is the default.

Superposition — two states coexisting as one

Decoherence — why cats don't blur

If superposition is real, why don't we see it in everyday life? Why isn't your coffee cup simultaneously on the table and on the floor?

The answer is decoherence. When a quantum system interacts with its environment — air molecules, photons, thermal vibrations — the delicate phase relationships that allow interference are destroyed, almost instantly. The superposition doesn't vanish; it leaks into the environment, becoming entangled with so many particles that it becomes, for all practical purposes, invisible.

Superposition is fragile. The larger and warmer the system, the faster it decoheres. An electron can remain in superposition for a long time. A cat cannot. Not because the physics is different — but because the cat is in conversation with trillions of particles, every single one of which is eavesdropping on its state.

The universe doesn't stop being quantum at the human scale. It just becomes very, very good at hiding it.

Chapter Four
Quantum Entanglement

Now for the strangest thing of all.

When two particles interact and then separate, their quantum states can become linked — entangled — in a way that has no classical analogue. Once entangled, the measurement of one particle instantly determines the state of the other, no matter how far apart they are.

Not "influences." Not "sends a signal." Determines. Instantaneously. Across any distance.

Entangled pair — correlated across distance

Einstein hated this. He called it "spukhafte Fernwirkung" — spooky action at a distance. He believed it proved quantum mechanics was incomplete, that there must be hidden variables, predetermined instructions the particles carried with them like sealed envelopes.

Bell's theorem

In 1964, John Stewart Bell devised a theorem — and an inequality — that could distinguish between Einstein's hidden variables and genuine quantum entanglement. If particles carry predetermined instructions, their correlations must obey a mathematical limit. If quantum mechanics is right, those limits will be violated.

The experiments have been performed, repeatedly, with increasing precision. The Bell inequalities are violated. Every time. Nature chose quantum mechanics over Einstein's intuition.

Entanglement does not transmit information faster than light.
You cannot use it to send a message — the outcomes appear random locally.
Only when you compare results do the correlations emerge.
The "spookiness" is in the correlation, not in communication.

What entanglement is — and isn't

Entanglement means two systems share a single quantum state that cannot be factored into independent parts. The whole is not the sum of its parts — the whole is all there is. The individual particles, taken alone, are incomplete descriptions of reality.

This is not telepathy. Not faster-than-light signalling. It is something more subtle and more profound: a demonstration that the universe, at its deepest level, is not made of independent objects with independent properties. It is relational. Things are defined not by what they are in isolation, but by how they are connected.

Separated in space, still part of a single whole. That is what entanglement is.

"The opposite of a correct statement is a false statement. But the opposite of a profound truth may well be another profound truth."
Niels Bohr
Chapter Five
The Wavefunction & Math

Behind all the strangeness is a single mathematical object that governs everything: the wavefunction, written Ψ (psi).

The wavefunction is a complex-valued function that assigns a probability amplitude to every possible configuration of a system. It doesn't tell you where a particle is. It tells you, at every point in space and time, the amplitude for it to be found there.

The Schrödinger equation

The evolution of the wavefunction in time is governed by the most important equation in quantum mechanics:

iℏ ∂Ψ/∂t = &Ĥ;Ψ
The Schrödinger equation — how quantum states evolve

On the left: how the wavefunction changes with time. On the right: the Hamiltonian operator Ĥ, which encodes the total energy of the system. This equation is deterministic — given the wavefunction at one moment, it predicts the wavefunction at every future moment with perfect precision.

The paradox: the evolution of possibilities is perfectly predictable. It is only when we observe — when we extract a definite outcome — that probability enters. The wavefunction evolves like clockwork. The measurement shatters the clockwork into chance.

Born's rule

Max Born discovered the link between the wavefunction and actual measurement: the probability of finding a particle at position x is given by the square of the amplitude.

P(x) = |Ψ(x)|²
Born's rule — probability from amplitude

This single rule bridges the abstract mathematics and the concrete laboratory. Amplitudes are complex numbers; probabilities are real. The wavefunction lives in an abstract space; Born's rule connects it to clicks on a detector and dots on a screen.

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle

The wavefunction reveals a fundamental limitation on what can be simultaneously known. The more precisely you know a particle's position, the less precisely you can know its momentum — and vice versa.

Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2
Heisenberg's uncertainty principle

This is not about imperfect instruments. It is not about disturbing the particle by measuring it. It is a property of nature itself. A particle with a perfectly defined position has a wavefunction that is a sharp spike — and a sharp spike, when decomposed into waves, requires an infinite spread of wavelengths. Definite position means utterly indefinite momentum. The two are mathematically conjugate — tightening one inevitably loosens the other.

Uncertainty is not ignorance. It is a structural feature of reality.
The universe does not have a simultaneous precise position and momentum to hide from us.
These properties, at that precision, simply do not co-exist.
Chapter Six
The Unified Picture

Now step back and see how every piece connects.

A quantum system exists in a quantum state — a mathematical description of all its possibilities. That state evolves smoothly in time according to the Schrödinger equation. While evolving, the system can be in a superposition of multiple outcomes simultaneously.

When the system interacts with a measuring device, one outcome is selected — and the probabilities of each outcome are given by Born's rule applied to the wavefunction. Before measurement, a particle is neither wave nor particle — it is described by a wavefunction that exhibits wave-particle duality, manifesting as one or the other depending on the experiment.

When two systems interact, they can become entangled — their states fused into a single, inseparable whole. And through it all, the uncertainty principle sets fundamental limits on what can be simultaneously known.

Quantum state
The complete description of what a system might do
Superposition
Being in multiple states at once — genuinely, not metaphorically
Wavefunction
The mathematical encoding of all superposed possibilities
Measurement
The act that collapses possibility into a single definite outcome
Duality
Wave and particle are complementary faces of the same thing
Entanglement
Two systems sharing a single state that cannot be separated
Uncertainty
Complementary properties cannot both be perfectly defined
Decoherence
How the quantum world hides itself at the classical scale

These are not separate theories. They are facets of a single, coherent framework — quantum mechanics — that has been tested more precisely than any other theory in the history of science. Its predictions have never been wrong.

The universe is not classical with quantum exceptions. It is quantum, with classical appearances.

Chapter Seven
Misconceptions

Quantum mechanics is the most misrepresented theory in popular culture. Here is what it does not say.

"Consciousness causes collapse"

This is the most persistent myth. The idea that human consciousness is somehow special in quantum mechanics — that it takes a mind to collapse a wavefunction. It doesn't. Any interaction with a sufficiently complex environment counts as a "measurement." A Geiger counter collapses a wavefunction. A photon bouncing off dust collapses a wavefunction. No mind required. Decoherence is physical, not mental.

"The observer effect means reality is subjective"

The observer effect in quantum mechanics is about physical interaction, not philosophical subjectivity. When we say "observation" changes a system, we mean that extracting information requires an interaction — a photon must bounce off an electron for you to "see" it, and that photon kicks the electron. The word "observer" is misleading. A better word would be "interactor."

"Quantum mechanics only applies to tiny things"

Quantum mechanics applies to everything. It doesn't switch off at a certain size. Superconductors, superfluids, neutron stars, and the large-scale structure of the universe all require quantum mechanics. The reason we don't see superpositions in everyday life is decoherence, not a breakdown of the laws.

"Entanglement allows faster-than-light communication"

It does not. When you measure one entangled particle, the other's state is determined — but you cannot control which outcome you get. Your measurement looks random. It is only when you compare notes with the other observer (which requires a classical, slower-than-light signal) that the correlations appear. Entanglement is correlation, not communication.

"Quantum mechanics is just probability"

Classical probability is about ignorance — a coin is heads or tails, you just don't know which. Quantum probability is fundamentally different. The amplitudes are complex numbers that interfere with each other. It is this interference — constructive, destructive, and everything between — that distinguishes quantum probability from classical uncertainty. Superposition is not coin-flipping. It is something without classical analogue.

The strangeness of quantum mechanics is real.
But it is precise, mathematical, and testable — not mystical.
When someone uses the word "quantum" to sell you something, be skeptical.
The universe is strange enough without our help.
Chapter Eight
Interpretations

The mathematics of quantum mechanics is unambiguous. It works. It predicts. It has never failed. But what it means — what is actually happening when a superposition collapses, what the wavefunction really is — is still debated.

These are the major interpretations. They agree on every prediction. They disagree on the nature of reality.

The Copenhagen Interpretation
Bohr, Heisenberg · 1920s
The wavefunction is a tool for calculating probabilities, not a description of reality itself. When a measurement occurs, the wavefunction "collapses" — and asking what the system was doing before measurement is meaningless. There is no deeper reality beneath the probabilities. The question "what is really happening?" has no answer. Shut up and calculate.
Many-Worlds Interpretation
Hugh Everett III · 1957
The wavefunction never collapses. Instead, every quantum measurement causes the universe to branch. In one branch, the electron went through slit A. In another, slit B. Both outcomes happen — both are equally real — in separate, non-communicating branches of an ever-splitting multiverse. There is no collapse, only the illusion of it from within each branch.
Pilot Wave Theory
De Broglie, David Bohm · 1927 / 1952
Particles are real, definite objects with real, definite positions at all times. But they are guided by a "pilot wave" — the wavefunction — which tells them where to go. The wave goes through both slits; the particle goes through one. The interference pattern arises because the pilot wave shapes the particle's trajectory. Deterministic, but non-local — the pilot wave depends on the entire configuration of the universe.
QBism (Quantum Bayesianism)
Fuchs, Schack · 2000s
The wavefunction doesn't describe the system — it describes the beliefs of the agent making measurements. Quantum mechanics is a user's manual for navigating an unpredictable world, not a mirror of reality. Probabilities are personal, not objective. The "collapse" is simply an update of beliefs, like revising your confidence after seeing evidence.
Relational Quantum Mechanics
Carlo Rovelli · 1996
Quantum states are not absolute — they are relative to the observer. A particle has properties only relative to another system that interacts with it. There is no "God's-eye view" of the quantum state. Reality is a web of interactions, and properties only exist in the context of relationships between systems.

No experiment has ever distinguished between these interpretations. They are, empirically, identical. The choice between them is — for now — a philosophical one, guided by what kind of strangeness you find most tolerable.

The universe gives us the answers. It does not tell us what they mean.

"I think I can safely say that nobody understands quantum mechanics."
Richard Feynman
Chapter Nine
Mental Model Summary

If you've read this far, you know more about quantum mechanics than most. Here is a framework for holding it all together.

The one-sentence version

Reality is fundamentally probabilistic, relational, and dual — and the tension between these properties is not a flaw, but the architecture.

The five truths

1. Nature keeps its options open. A quantum system does not commit to a definite state until forced to by interaction. Before that, all allowed possibilities coexist — as superposition.

2. Measurement is participation. You cannot passively observe a quantum system. To observe is to interact, and interaction changes the system. The boundary between observer and observed is blurred.

3. Complementary properties are exclusive. Some pairs of properties — position and momentum, wave and particle — cannot be simultaneously precise. Gaining certainty about one necessarily means losing it about the other. This is structural, not circumstantial.

4. Connection is real. Entangled systems are a single system, regardless of distance. The universe is not made of independent parts with independent properties. It is made of relationships.

5. The classical world is an approximation. The smooth, definite, predictable world of everyday experience is an emergent consequence of decoherence — the quantum world interacting with itself at such enormous scale that the strangeness averages out. But it never disappears.

How to think about it

Don't try to visualise quantum systems as tiny versions of big things. They aren't. Electrons are not little balls. Photons are not little bullets or little water waves. They are quantum objects — a category of thing that exists only at the quantum scale and has no direct analogue in human-scale experience.

The mathematics is the most honest description we have. The words — "wave," "particle," "collapse," "spin" — are metaphors borrowed from a classical world that doesn't apply. Use them as handles, not as truths.

And when the metaphors break — when the words stop making sense — you are not failing to understand. You are understanding correctly. This is what understanding quantum mechanics feels like.

Chapter Ten
Learning Roadmap

If this has made you curious — and it should — here is a path forward. From intuition to rigor, from wonder to understanding.

1
Build Intuition
Start with narrative and thought experiments. Understand what happens before asking why. Get comfortable with the strangeness — don't rush past it.
Read: "QED" by Feynman · Watch: 3Blue1Brown's "Some light quantum mechanics"
2
Learn the Language
Complex numbers. Linear algebra — vectors, matrices, eigenvalues. This is the vocabulary of quantum mechanics. Without it, every explanation is an approximation. With it, the theory becomes shockingly simple.
Read: "No-Nonsense Quantum Mechanics" by Schwichtenberg · Khan Academy linear algebra
3
The Formal Framework
Hilbert spaces, operators, commutation relations, the postulates of quantum mechanics. This is where you learn the real theory — not interpretations, not metaphors, but the axioms and their consequences.
Read: "Introduction to Quantum Mechanics" by Griffiths · MIT 8.04 OpenCourseWare
4
Applications & Experiments
Quantum computing, quantum cryptography, quantum teleportation, Bose-Einstein condensates, the quantum Hall effect. See how the theory connects to technology and experiment.
Read: "Quantum Computing: An Applied Approach" by Hidary · IBM Qiskit tutorials
5
Philosophy & Foundations
Once you know the theory, return to the interpretations with new eyes. The measurement problem. The ontology of the wavefunction. Whether quantum mechanics is complete. These questions are open — and you will now be equipped to think about them seriously.
Read: "Something Deeply Hidden" by Sean Carroll · "Helgoland" by Carlo Rovelli

The path is long. But it starts with what you've already done — with curiosity, and with the willingness to be uncomfortable. The universe is not obligated to make sense on our terms. But it does reward those who look closely.

Duality is real.

Not a theory. Not a compromise. Not a flaw in our understanding. The most fundamental fabric of the universe insists on being two things at once.

A photon is a wave and a particle. A state is all possibilities and one outcome. Two particles, separated by the width of the universe, can be a single whole.

Things that seem to oppose each other can complete each other. Things that seem contradictory can both be true. The deepest truths are not resolved — they are held.

Some truths can only be whole when they are held as two.
